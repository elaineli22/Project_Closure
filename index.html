Project Methodology Explanation

‚ñº

Requirements: Deliverables are well-defined (certificate, case study, dashboard, portfolio), but dataset choice, dashboard design, and integration still require iteration and feedback.

Delivery Cadence: To reduce risk, the project is divided into three major releases instead of one final delivery.

  • Release 1 (Week 1-12): Certification
  • Release 2 (Week 8-10): Skill Analysis
  • Release 3 (Week 11-13): Portfolio Website
  • Release 4 (Week 14): Job Application

Approach:

  • Incremental: Portfolio built up progressively, finalized at semester end.

Why Hybrid? This approach balances predictability with adaptability‚Äîideal for a semester project that combines fixed requirements with evolving creative elements.

Project Tool Selection Explanation

‚ñº

Tools Explored

  • Microsoft Project ‚Üí Strong for predictive and hybrid; detailed scheduling/reporting, but heavy and paid-only.

Final Choice: Microsoft Project

Microsoft Project supports hybrid methodology by enabling structured releases and sprints while using feedback cycles. It‚Äôs more customizable than Trello, more versatile than Jira, and has a free plan that fits a semester project.

Time management and milestone achievement

Situation: In my Network Security Essentials & Practice class, I had an individual research paper on the Morris Worm, one of the first computer worms to impact the internet. Due to a busy month filled with other assignments and midterm preparations, I fell behind schedule and realized I only had a few days left to complete the entire paper.


Task: I needed to manage my time efficiently and organize my work so I could complete the research, analysis, and writing before the submission deadline without taking shortcuts or using less effort to complete a task quickly.


Action: I started by breaking the project into smaller tasks or milestones. For example, I would dedicate 2-3 hours for gathering credible sources, another 2-3 hours for outlining and drafting, and the next day for editing and proofreading. I used Excel spreadsheets to manage my schedule and stay focused on working with short breaks in between. I prioritized using academic or scholarly articles that directly supported my claims. I also minimized distractions by silencing notifications and setting up a dedicated workspace.


Result: I submitted my paper on time and received a satisfactory grade. I learned to stay calm under pressure and realized how having a structured time management schedule can turn a potentially stressful situation into a successful outcome.

Team collaboration and communication strategies

Team collaboration and communication strategies

Situation: In one of my classes, our team had a final project presentation that required consistent collaboration over several weeks. However, our group members all had conflicting schedules due to part-time jobs, extracurricular activities, and personal responsibilities. It became difficult to coordinate meetings, share progress, and ensure that everyone contributed equally.


Task: I took the initiative to improve our coordination and make sure that everyone could contribute and meet deadlines despite our conflicting schedules.


Action: I created a shared project timeline using Excel spreadsheets to outline tasks, due dates, and who was responsible for each task. Then, I set up a group chat and weekly virtual check-ins so members who couldn‚Äôt meet in person could still participate. I also set up feedback sessions so if someone needed help, they could reach out. We all had a common goal of delivering a finished project that reflected our efforts.


Result: At the end, we managed to stay on schedule and complete our project. We realized that clear communication and flexibility are important in group projects. We learned how to coordinate and collaborate when team members have conflicting schedules or different personalities.

Be a diligent, respectful, and caring steward: Ethical Decision Under Pressure

‚ñº

Be a diligent, respectful, and caring steward: Ethical Decision Under Pressure

Situation: In my junior year, I worked in a group project where we analyzed datasets and used Tableau for data visualizations and Power BI for data cleaning. During the project, one of my teammates discovered that removing certain outlier data points made our visualizations look much cleaner and more favorable to our conclusions. Another team member recommended excluding those points to make the presentation look better.


Task: I realized this was not an ethical decision. We shouldn‚Äôt present an incomplete but cleaner dataset and maintain integrity by including all valid data, even if it made our findings more complex. My task was to help the team make a responsible decision without causing conflict or compromising our credibility.


Action: I initiated a team meeting about data ethics and reminded the team that the purpose of our project was to present accurate analysis, not a biased one. I explained how excluding valid data could mislead our audience and undermine the reliability of our results. I suggested keeping the full dataset and explaining the outliers in our presentation and discussing their potential impact which allowed us to maintain transparency.


Result: Our team realized we needed to make this ethical decision by including all data points with explanations in our presentation. We maintained ethical integrity even under pressure to impress, which builds long-term trust and credibility.

Create a collaborative project team environment: Team Conflict Resolution

‚ñº

Create a collaborative project team environment: Team Conflict Resolution

Situation: In my Decision Supports Systems class, I worked with a team of five students on our final project. We were analyzing the implementation of a smart tagging system, using tools like Excel, Tableau, and Power BI to generate descriptive, predictive, and prescriptive findings. Halfway through the project, we faced a major conflict where two teammates strongly disagreed on which tool to use for our prescriptive analysis. One preferred Excel for its simplicity, while the other wanted to use Power BI to make better visualizations. This made it hard to collaborate and started slowing down our progress.


Task: The goal was to resolve the disagreement and help the team refocus on collaboration rather than personal tool preferences.


Action: We communicated the reasonings for each member‚Äôs tool preference and how their preferred tools could be used to analyze the dataset. This helped everyone see how each tool could be used for our project. We compromised on using Excel for the initial data cleaning and Power BI for visualization and predictive modeling.


Result: The compromise worked well, and progress was back on track. We created a comprehensive report that showed how we could use multiple tools to complete the project.

Focus on Value: Realigning to Value

‚ñº

Focus on Value: Realigning to Value

Situation: During my junior year in college, I worked with my team of three students in developing a simple mobile app interface for a coffee shop. The goal was to help customers browse the menu, place orders, and see daily specials more easily.


Task: Halfway through the project, I noticed that our focus was shifting. We started adding features like loyalty points and push notifications, which are things the cafe did not need at the time. The development time was increasing, and we needed to finish making the ordering experience simple and efficient for customers.


Action: I initiated a quick feedback session to review what was needed. Making the app simple and easy to use was what mattered the most, so we re-prioritized our application by removing extra features and improving the menu navigation, load times, and visual design. We also had short weekly check-ins to ensure things were progressing on track.


Result: We finished the app on schedule. The app was simple, functional, and exactly what was needed for a new coffee shop. The experience taught me the importance of continuously checking in with my team members on project progress.
